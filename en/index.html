About the Laboratory

The Soft Matter Physics Laboratory at Yamaguchi University is dedicated to elucidating the physical mechanisms underlying collective and nonequilibrium behaviors in soft matter systems such as colloidal dispersions, polymers, and liquids, based on concepts of statistical physics and condensed matter physics.

Our research particularly focuses on molecular-scale electronic states, local structures, and dynamics in liquids and solutions. We promote an integrated research approach combining advanced spectroscopic experiments, theoretical analysis, and numerical simulations.

By employing state-of-the-art soft X-ray emission spectroscopy, we experimentally investigate electronic structures and intermolecular interactions of molecules in liquid environments that are difficult to access by conventional techniques. Through these studies, we aim to uncover universal physical principles governing collective motion, phase separation, and nonequilibrium processes in soft matter.

Through fundamental research, our laboratory seeks to provide a theoretical foundation for physical chemistry, interface science, and materials science, while also contributing to the development and standardization of new experimental methodologies.

Research Topics

Development of Soft X-ray Spectroscopy for Electronic Structure Analysis

We develop experimental and analytical techniques based on soft X-ray emission and absorption spectroscopy (XES/XAS) to probe electronic states of atoms and molecules in liquids and solutions with high energy resolution.

Soft X-ray spectroscopy provides elemental selectivity and local sensitivity, enabling direct evaluation of local electronic structures and chemical bonding that are difficult to access by optical spectroscopy or neutron scattering.

To overcome experimental challenges specific to liquid samples, we optimize measurement conditions and data analysis methods, thereby expanding the applicability of soft X-ray spectroscopy to liquid-phase systems.

Applications to Molecular Liquids and Solution Chemistry

Using the developed spectroscopic techniques, we investigate electronic states, hydrogen-bond networks, and solvation structures of solute molecules in aqueous and organic solvents.

Current research topics include electronic structure analysis of ionic liquids and solvated ionic liquids, as well as solvent-dependent electronic structure variations in organic and carboxylic acid molecules.

These studies aim to establish an electronic-level understanding of how intermolecular interactions govern reactivity, thermodynamic stability, and dynamic behavior of molecular systems.

Extension to Interfaces and Electrochemical Reactions

We are extending soft X-ray spectroscopy to investigate material interfaces and interfacial reactions, particularly at electrode/solution interfaces.

By developing in situ observation techniques for electronic states during electrochemical reactions, we aim to achieve molecular-level understanding of reaction fields that have traditionally been treated as black boxes. These studies are expected to contribute to research on secondary batteries, catalysis, and functional interface materials.

Related Studies on Polymer Structure Formation

As part of externally funded projects, we also study nanoscale hierarchical structure formation in polymer systems, such as bottlebrush polymers.

By integrating electronic-state information with structural analysis, we aim to deepen the understanding of self-organization mechanisms in polymeric soft matter systems and explore new applications of soft X-ray spectroscopy.

Conceptual Framework

Our research is centered on the advancement of electronic-state measurement and analysis based on soft X-ray spectroscopy and its application to liquids, solutions, and interfacial systems.

By applying advanced spectroscopic techniques to systems that are difficult to access experimentally, we aim to clarify the essential mechanisms of intermolecular interactions, hydrogen bonding, and solvation structures.

Through these efforts, we seek to strengthen the theoretical foundation of nonequilibrium soft matter physics and interfacial science, promoting integrated research across experiments, theory, and computation.
